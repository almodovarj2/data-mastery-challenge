# The Data Mastery Challenge

### What is it?

The Data Mastery Challenge is a 180-day ultralearning experiment focused on financial analytics, predictive modeling, and applied data science.

The goal is to become a more complete data professional through deliberate practice, project-based learning, and publicly documenting everything I learn and build along the way.

### Why?

In 2026, the bar for data professionals has risen dramatically. The skills that made data professionals valuable just a few years ago have become commoditized. To stand out now, you need to offer something **more**. Being good, or even great, is no longer enough. You have to be *exceptional*.

I’m especially interested in the intersection of data, finance, risk, forecasting, and decision-making. Banks, credit unions, insurance companies, and financial institutions rely heavily on data to understand customers, manage risk, detect problems, forecast outcomes, and make better decisions.

That makes finance a strong domain for this challenge: technical enough to demand serious skill, practical enough to matter, and stable enough to reward deep domain knowledge.

I've been a long-time fan of Scott H. Young’s *Ultralearning* and his famous MIT Challenge, as well as Cal Newport’s work on deep work, craft, and focused intensity. For that reason, I've decided to create my own ultralearning/deep work challenge to see what kind of progress I can make in half a year.

### The Goal

This is my public commitment to see how much progress I can make through deliberate, intense practice.

The goal is not to collect certificates, finish courses, or passively consume material. The goal is to build skill through output.

Over the next 180 days, I plan to build a portfolio of projects focused on applied financial analytics, including areas like:

- SQL for financial and operational analysis
- Python for data cleaning, analysis, and modeling
- Statistics and probability
- Regression analysis
- Predictive modeling
- Forecasting
- Credit risk analysis
- Fraud and anomaly detection
- Portfolio and customer segmentation
- Financial dashboards and decision-support tools
- Data quality and model validation
- AI-assisted analytical workflows
- Technical writing and documentation
- End-to-end data products

The challenge is simple:

**Learn deeply. Build constantly. Document honestly. Improve relentlessly.**

### Guiding Principles

**1. Build in public.**  
I will document what I learn, what I build, where I struggle, and how my thinking changes over time.

**2. Practice deliberately.**  
The point is not just to spend time studying. The point is to identify weaknesses, attack them directly, and create feedback loops.

**3. Prioritize projects over passive learning.**  
Books and courses matter, but only if they translate into applied work. Every resource should eventually become a project, exercise, article, technical note, model, dashboard, or case study.

**4. Focus on fundamentals.**  
Tools change. Fundamentals compound. SQL, statistics, modeling, business judgment, clear writing, and data storytelling will remain central.

**5. Build domain knowledge.**  
Technical skill matters more when it is attached to a real domain. For this challenge, I’m focusing heavily on finance, banking, risk, credit, forecasting, and decision support.

**6. Stay flexible.**  
This challenge is structured, but not rigid. Some weeks will be heavier than others. Some topics may need more time. The goal is consistent progress, not perfect adherence to a schedule.

**7. Document honestly.**  
I want this to be useful, not performative. That means documenting mistakes, false starts, confusion, and revisions alongside the wins.

### What I’m Building Toward

By the end of the first 180 days, I want to have:

- Solved a large volume of SQL and data interview-style problems
- Built multiple financial analytics projects
- Completed Python projects involving cleaning, analysis, modeling, and forecasting
- Built practical statistics, regression, and predictive modeling projects
- Created dashboards for financial, risk, and operational decision-making
- Built at least one end-to-end financial analytics product or case study
- Written clear documentation for every major project
- Published regular progress updates
- Built a public body of work that reflects real improvement
